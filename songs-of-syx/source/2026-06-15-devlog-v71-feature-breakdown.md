## Family
We now have proper propagation for all species that do propagate. A family tree: you can see a subject has a baby, 5 years of age, running around somewhere; you can double click and get to the baby. Almost everyone has babies — in population you have almost 2,000 children running around in this 8,000 total city. They take a while to grow up. You still have immigration as usual, and immigration is a lot easier to handle. The game hasn't become harder.

Kids inherit their parents' name. They only have one parent, because we don't have genders in Songs of Syx (genders would be a pain to implement properly). So they have one parent, and they have siblings and such.

When a subject becomes impregnated they incubate the baby for a few days and they don't work. So it's a big strain on your economy. Then the child grows up and becomes the children we know, and they run around and create havoc. If you want to keep them alive, you build a nursery and they spend their time there.

Settings for natural propagation: you set a limit, you can force them to breed faster, and you can force them to stop breeding at all (by setting the level) — that makes it forbidden to have children and they don't like that.

This new thing creates a different game — a game where you struggle to keep up with your growing population. There are tools to manage that.

## Education
Education has been overhauled. Schools and universities as usual, but different now: you invest a lot of resources into education and hopefully get rewarded; it's not that straightforward anymore. In the library/university you set limits for every type of subject; the highest limit is a tech that you unlock.

Educations are separate: children have child education; they start school at the time they would normally grow up, and if schools are allowed for them, they remain children until they finish school. You can't recoup that education later with universities — universities are separate. You need both for max education.

Education is mainly for technology now.

## Tech tree
The tech tree has been remade. Three points of knowledge: administration, innovation, and knowledge. Administration has been repurposed into being a tech-generating building. You use administration points mainly for your empire techs.

## Law and Order
This creates a new dimension of gameplay: you can be a liberal or a tyrant, and you control this by law. It's supposed to be a little bit easier to be a tyrant — that should offset the difficulty from having to support children.

At the start of the game everything is allowed; everyone has total freedom. If you have enough fulfillment, no one will want to do anything bad — they won't steal because they'll have everything; they're so happy they don't commit crime. Once they start committing crimes, your options are to increase fulfillment/happiness, or to forbid and punish crimes.

Every crime has severity (murder very severe; flashing not that severe). Murder is the most appreciated crime to punish initially — citizens won't react much to punishing murderers because they kind of like that. For every crime and punishment you get something called tyranny — robbing your citizens of absolute freedom. Criticism is the lowest form of crime; you won't get a benefit from punishing criticism early on. But if you have enough law facilities — guards, guard posts, execution squares, and the new monuments — cluttered around your city and a proper guard force, then it becomes beneficial to punish even criticism. That makes you a complete tyrant.

In an average game: if you set a harsh punishment you lose some happiness, but you get a net gain of loyalty because they're afraid of you and respect you through law. Loyalty is often higher than happiness, and increasingly higher the more you dabble into law.

The biggest factor in law is your guard force. You train your troops normally and then assign them to active duty; the active-duty ones count as your guards. They walk around the city, tend guard posts, march around in formation.

Plebeians: up to you — utopia route or tyranny route. Slaves have quite low fulfillment to begin with (you took away almost all their freedom), which means law is a lot more potent for slaves. You basically have to be a tyrant to own slaves. Captives are separate — you can do whatever you want with them; it only affects chivalry and cruelty (small fulfillment thing; the plan is to expand this to affect diplomatic relationships).

## Slavery
Slaves are a tradable good — you can import and export. But traded ones are not actually slaves, they're captives; you have to enslave the captives if you want them as slaves.

One combination of gameplay styles is NOT viable: Dondorian as a tyrant, because Dondorians rely on immigration. As a Dondorian tyrant the only route is probably to import Dondorian captives and free them to grow your population — unknown how feasible. Dondorians are going to get a special building for their propagation in the future, but not this version. Beware of Dondorian tyrant runs.

## Trade and more tech
The economy has always been unfair to certain industries — bread possibly the worst offender: exporting bread was simply worse than anything else. Tweaks made so every industry is viable to sustain your empire late game. The AI has "game theory": it predicts that bread sucks to export and won't invest heavily in it. AI also accounts for logistics costs — bread has very high logistics overhead, so bread is more expensive to trade and you earn more from exporting it.

The tech tree makes every industry viable, including importing raw materials: importing grain and exporting bread is viable but quite a bit more expensive — first tech up farming, then the refiner. At the end of the tech tree are consumption nodes; every industry with inputs has these. Upgrading them means using less grain than usual for the bread. Fully teched, importing grain and exporting bread is actually the most profitable industry you can do.

Tech tree costs halved (~50%) for every tree — easier, but trade might be a little less profitable; those two should even each other out.

Trade UI: green and red arrows — exporting rations pays this amount; import something with fewer arrows and you make a profit.

Work preferences were adjusted — alpha testers put in the values.

## Game modes
Preset game modes with different offsets (what we had before, but preset, with icons). Each game mode has its own achievement.

## Death monuments
New statues by Orange Blue Hue (gargoyles), skull mounds, heads on spikes. With a lot of law you can increase your guard force so you don't need to spend resources on expensive services — the city looks dystopian, citizens in a bad situation, but they can't do anything because they fear you.

## Decrees
Automatic decrees: e.g. to farm slaves, set persecution, capture with guards, and turn them into slaves. Or cannibalism — a steady supply of meat and other resources.

## Secret police
The old slaver building repurposed into a secret police building — basically a torture chamber. The final stage of tyranny: torture your people to find traitors and increase terror and obedience.
